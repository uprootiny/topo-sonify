What Does It Mean to Sonify Topology?

This project takes a geometric point cloud in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), constructs its Vietoris-Rips simplicial complex at varying scale parameters, computes the persistent homology of the resulting filtration, and maps the topological invariants into audio synthesis parameters in real time. The result is a sound that is the topology: you hear connected components as distinct voices, loops as cyclic modulation, voids as resonant sub-oscillators, and the Euler characteristic as the reverb character of the acoustic space itself.

The phrase "topology-first" is deliberate. Most data sonification systems begin with a metaphor—"pitch = magnitude," "pan = x-coordinate"—and then search for data to fill the metaphor. We reverse this. We begin with the mathematics: a simplicial complex \(K\), its chain groups \(C_k(K; \mathbb{F}_2)\), the boundary operators \(\partial_k\), the homology groups \(H_k(K; \mathbb{F}_2)\), and the persistence diagram \(\mathrm{Dgm}(K)\). Only then do we ask: what sound does this invariant want to become?

The answer emerges from the structure of the invariants themselves. The Betti number \(\beta_0\) counts connected components—discrete, independent entities—so it maps naturally to independent oscillator voices in a chord. The first Betti number \(\beta_1\) counts independent cycles, and cycles are inherently periodic, so \(\beta_1\) maps to the depth of cyclic (LFO) modulation. The Euler characteristic \(\chi = \sum (-1)^k \beta_k\), a single integer encoding global curvature via the Gauss-Bonnet theorem, controls the reverb character: positive curvature (sphere-like) yields short, focused reverb, while negative curvature (hyperbolic) yields long, diffuse reverb.

Intellectual History

From Euler to Poincaré

The story begins with Leonhard Euler's 1736 solution of the Königsberg bridge problem, the first theorem in graph theory and arguably the first theorem in topology. Euler observed that the possibility of crossing every bridge exactly once depends not on the geometry of the bridges but on a combinatorial invariant: the number of vertices with odd degree. This is the first instance of a topological argument—an argument that depends on connectivity, not on metric properties.

In the same letter, Euler proved what we now call the Euler formula for polyhedra: \[ V - E + F = 2, \] where \(V\), \(E\), \(F\) are the numbers of vertices, edges, and faces of a convex polyhedron. The remarkable fact—that this alternating sum is always 2, regardless of the polyhedron—makes \(\chi = V - E + F\) the first topological invariant.

Henri Poincaré's 1895 Analysis Situs generalized Euler's formula to arbitrary dimensions by introducing homology groups. Poincaré defined the Betti numbers \(\beta_k\) (named after Enrico Betti, who had earlier generalized Euler's idea to higher connectivity numbers) and proved the Euler-Poincaré theorem: \[ \chi(K) = \sum_{k=0}^{d} (-1)^k \beta_k = \sum_{k=0}^{d} (-1)^k f_k, \] relating the purely combinatorial \(f\)-vector \((f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_d)\) to the algebraic Betti numbers. This equation is the bridge between the combinatorial and the algebraic, and it is central to our sonification design: the \(f\)-vector gives us harmonic ratios, while the Betti numbers give us voices and modulation.

Computational Topology and TDA

The modern era begins with Edelsbrunner, Letscher, and Zomorodian's 2002 paper introducing persistent homology, which solved a fundamental problem: given a point cloud, at what scale should one build the simplicial complex? Their answer: build it at all scales simultaneously, track how topological features are born and die, and record the result as a persistence diagram.

The key theoretical result is the stability theorem (Cohen-Steiner, Edelsbrunner, Harer, 2007): small perturbations to the input produce small perturbations to the persistence diagram, as measured by the bottleneck distance. This makes persistence diagrams robust descriptors of shape, suitable for real-time interactive systems where the point cloud is continuously changing.

Topological Data Analysis (TDA) has since found applications in materials science, neuroscience, genomics, sensor networks, and machine learning. Our contribution is to extend TDA into the domain of sound: using persistent homology not to analyze data but to generate audio, creating a direct perceptual channel from topological structure to human hearing.

Why Sound?

The human auditory system is exquisitely sensitive to structural properties that have natural topological analogues. We perceive:

  • Polyphony (multiple independent voices) — analogous to \(\beta_0\), counting independent components.
  • Periodicity (rhythmic and timbral modulation) — analogous to \(\beta_1\), counting independent cycles.
  • Spatial depth (reverb, room acoustics) — analogous to curvature, encoded in \(\chi\) via Gauss-Bonnet.
  • Harmonic structure (overtone series, just intonation) — analogous to the \(f\)-vector, counting simplices at each dimension.
  • Temporal evolution (notes appearing and fading) — analogous to persistence pairs, marking birth and death of features.

Sound is, in a precise mathematical sense, the right target for topological sonification: the signal space \(L^2([0,T])\) is an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space with a rich inner product structure, and the Fourier basis provides a spectral decomposition analogous to the eigendecomposition of the combinatorial Laplacian on the simplicial complex.

Mathematical Prerequisites

These notes assume familiarity with:

  • Linear algebra over finite fields, particularly \(\mathbb{F}_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\). We use matrix reduction (Gaussian elimination) over \(\mathbb{F}_2\) to compute ranks, kernels, and images of boundary matrices.
  • Abstract algebra: groups, quotient groups \(G/H\), free abelian groups. The homology group \(H_k = Z_k / B_k\) is a quotient of the cycle group by the boundary group.
  • Point-set topology: open sets, continuity, compactness, connectedness. We use these notions informally; the simplicial setting discretizes them.
  • Discrete signal processing: sampling, the DFT, \(z\)-transforms, difference equations. The audio modules implement standard DSP algorithms (biquad filters, delay lines, waveshapers) parameterized by topological invariants.

No background in algebraic topology is assumed. Chapters 1–3 develop the theory from scratch, with full proofs of the key results. The code in src/topology/ implements exactly the mathematics described in the text.

Signal Chain Architecture

The system is implemented in Rust, compiled to WebAssembly, and run in the browser via the Web Audio API. The signal chain flows from topology through invariants to instruments to effects to output:

TOPOLOGY LAYER ============== Point Cloud ──► Vietoris-Rips ──► SimplicialComplex K {p₁,...,pₙ} at radius r (src/topology/complex.rs) INVARIANT LAYER =============== K ──► f-vector (f₀, f₁, ..., f_d) ──► harmonic ratios K ──► Betti numbers (β₀, β₁, β₂) ──► voice/modulation/sub config K ──► Euler characteristic χ ──► curvature / reverb K ──► Persistence diagram Dgm ──► delay taps + Morse score INSTRUMENT LAYER ================ BettiDrone ─────────┐ β₀ oscillators, β₁ LFOs, β₂ subs SimplicialPluck ────┤ Karplus-Strong with f-vector excitation MorseSynth ─────────┘ persistence pairs → temporal score EFFECTS LAYER ============= ──► BettiDistortion ──► PersistenceDelay ──► CurvatureReverb ──► OUTPUT ====== ──► clamp(-1, 1) ──► Web Audio AudioWorklet ──► DAC ──► speakers

Each layer is detailed in a subsequent chapter. Chapter 1 develops the topology layer (simplicial complexes). Chapter 2 develops the invariant layer (homology and Betti numbers). Chapter 3 develops the persistence layer (filtrations and persistence diagrams). Chapter 4 develops the instrument and effect layers (sonification mapping).

The Code

The Rust source lives in src/ with the following module structure: 

// src/lib.rs — crate root
pub mod topology;    // simplicial complexes, homology, persistence
pub mod audio;       // oscillators, filters, effects
pub mod instruments; // BettiDrone, SimplicialPluck, MorseSynth
pub mod mapping;     // TopologySonifier — the central engine

// src/topology/

// src/audio/
//   oscillator.rs — Waveform, Oscillator with polyBLEP

// src/mapping/
//   sonifier.rs — TopologySonifier: the central pipeline

Each subsequent chapter includes the relevant code stitches, with line-by-line commentary connecting the implementation to the mathematical definitions. The code and the math are developed together: the math justifies the code, and the code makes the math computable.

How to Read These Notes

These notes are designed for a graduate seminar. Each chapter opens with motivation, develops the mathematical theory with full definitions and proofs, and then presents the corresponding Rust implementation with detailed commentary. Exercises at the end of each chapter range from straightforward computations to open-ended explorations.

The intended reading order follows the chapter numbering:

  1. Simplicial Complexes — the fundamental discrete model of space.
  2. Homology — the algebraic machinery for detecting holes.
  3. Persistent Homology — the multi-scale refinement.
  4. Sonification Mapping — the creative/engineering design.
  5. Storyboard — the interaction and architecture design.

Readers with a background in algebraic topology may skip to Chapter 4 directly. Readers interested only in the audio engineering may read Chapter 4 independently, referring back as needed.

Remark (Notation)

Throughout these notes, \(K\) denotes a simplicial complex, \(\sigma\) denotes a simplex, \(\partial_k\) denotes the \(k\)-th boundary operator, \(H_k\) denotes the \(k\)-th homology group, \(\beta_k = \mathrm{rank}(H_k)\) denotes the \(k\)-th Betti number, \(\chi\) denotes the Euler characteristic, and \(\mathrm{Dgm}\) denotes a persistence diagram. All coefficients are in \(\mathbb{F}_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\) unless stated otherwise. Audio signals are elements of \(L^2([0,T])\) sampled at 44100 Hz.
