Chapter 4: The Sonification Mapping

From topological invariants to audio synthesis

4.1 The Fundamental Question

Given a simplicial complex \(K\) with its Betti numbers \(\beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2\), \(f\)-vector \(\mathbf{f}\), Euler characteristic \(\chi\), and persistence diagram \(\mathrm{Dgm}(K)\), we must design a mapping: \[ \Phi : \{\text{topological invariants}\} \longrightarrow L^2([0, T]) \] from the space of invariants to the space of audio signals. The mapping must be:

  • Faithful: distinct topological structures should produce perceptually distinct sounds.
  • Continuous: small changes to the topology should produce small changes to the sound (guaranteed by the stability theorem for persistence-dependent parameters).
  • Interpretable: a trained listener should be able to infer topological properties from the sound alone.

4.2 Signal Spaces

Definition 4.1 (Signal space)

The space of square-integrable audio signals on \([0, T]\) is the Hilbert space: \[ L^2([0, T]) = \left\{ s : [0, T] \to \mathbb{R} \;\bigg|\; \int_0^T |s(t)|^2 \, dt < \infty \right\} \] with inner product \(\langle s_1, s_2 \rangle = \int_0^T s_1(t) s_2(t) \, dt\). The Fourier basis \(\{e^{2\pi i n t / T}\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}\) provides an orthonormal basis, and the Fourier coefficients \(\hat{s}(n)\) give the frequency-domain representation.

In discrete time (sample rate \(f_s = 44100\) Hz), our signals live in \(\mathbb{R}^N\) where \(N = f_s \cdot T\). The sonification mapping \(\Phi\) is implemented as a real-time function that produces one sample at a time: 

/// Generate the next audio sample through the full signal chain.
/// This is the core of Phi: invariants -> L^2([0,T]).
pub fn next_sample(&mut self) -> f64 {
    // Instrument mix (topology -> oscillation)
    let drone_out = self.drone.next_sample() * self.drone_level;
    let pluck_out = self.pluck.next_sample() * self.pluck_level;
    let morse_out = self.morse.next_sample() * self.morse_level;

    let dry = drone_out + pluck_out + morse_out;

    // Effects chain (topology -> spatial/spectral processing)
    let distorted = self.distortion.process(dry);
    let delayed = self.delay.process(distorted);
    let reverbed = self.reverb.process(delayed);

    (reverbed * self.master_volume).clamp(-1.0, 1.0)
}

4.3 The Combinatorial Laplacian and Spectral Graph Theory

The mapping between topology and sound has a deep mathematical foundation in spectral graph theory. The combinatorial Laplacian of a simplicial complex provides the bridge.

Definition 4.2 (Combinatorial \(k\)-Laplacian)

The \(k\)-th combinatorial Laplacian (or Hodge Laplacian) of a simplicial complex \(K\) is the linear operator: \[ L_k = \partial_{k+1} \partial_{k+1}^T + \partial_k^T \partial_k : C_k \to C_k. \] This is a positive semidefinite operator. Its kernel is isomorphic to the homology group: \[ \ker(L_k) \cong H_k(K; \mathbb{R}). \] This is the discrete Hodge theorem.

Theorem 4.3 (Discrete Hodge decomposition)

Every \(k\)-chain \(c \in C_k\) has a unique orthogonal decomposition: \[ c = c_{\mathrm{harm}} + \partial_{k+1} c' + \partial_k^T c'', \] where \(c_{\mathrm{harm}} \in \ker(L_k)\) is a harmonic chain (a cycle that is "as smooth as possible"), \(\partial_{k+1} c' \in \mathrm{im}(\partial_{k+1})\) is a boundary component, and \(\partial_k^T c'' \in \mathrm{im}(\partial_k^T)\) is a "coboundary" component.

The eigenvalues of \(L_k\) are non-negative: \(0 = \lambda_0 \leq \lambda_1 \leq \cdots\). The number of zero eigenvalues equals \(\beta_k\). The nonzero eigenvalues determine the "natural frequencies" of oscillation on the complex—exactly analogous to the eigenfrequencies of a vibrating membrane or string.

Remark 4.4

For the graph Laplacian (\(k = 0\)), \(L_0 = D - A\) where \(D\) is the degree matrix and \(A\) is the adjacency matrix. The smallest nonzero eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) (the Fiedler value or algebraic connectivity) measures how well-connected the graph is. In our sonification, higher connectivity produces richer harmonic content because the eigenmodes are more densely spaced.

4.4 The Mapping Design

We now describe each component of the mapping in detail.

4.4.1 \(\beta_0 \to\) Oscillator Count (BettiDrone)

Mapping M1: Components to Voices

Each connected component of \(K\) is an independent topological entity. The BettiDrone creates \(\max(\beta_0, 1)\) independent oscillator voices. Each voice is a sawtooth oscillator (maximum spectral content: all harmonics) with slight detuning \(\Delta f_i = f_0 \cdot (1 + 0.002 \cdot i)\) to create the chorus effect of beating frequencies.

4.4.2 \(\beta_1 \to\) Modulation Depth (LFOs)

Mapping M2: Loops to Modulation

Each independent loop in \(H_1(K)\) creates a low-frequency oscillator (LFO). The \(i\)-th LFO has frequency \(f_{\mathrm{LFO},i} = 0.1 + 0.07i\) Hz (sub-audio rates). The modulation depth of each LFO is \(m_i = 0.003 \cdot (1 + i)\), applied as frequency modulation to the drone: \[ f_{\mathrm{mod}}(t) = f_0 \cdot \left(1 + \sum_{i=0}^{\beta_1 - 1} m_i \sin(2\pi f_{\mathrm{LFO},i} \cdot t)\right). \] With \(\beta_1 = 0\), the drone is static. With \(\beta_1 = 1\), it gently vibrates. With \(\beta_1 \geq 2\), complex modulation patterns emerge from the interference of multiple LFOs at incommensurate frequencies.

4.4.3 \(\beta_2 \to\) Sub-Oscillators (Resonant Cavities)

Mapping M3: Voids to Sub-Oscillators

Each enclosed void (element of \(H_2(K)\)) adds a sine sub-oscillator at \(f_0 / 2\) (one octave below), with slight detuning between subs. The acoustic metaphor: a void is an enclosed resonant cavity, like the body of a guitar or the bell of a trumpet. These cavities reinforce the fundamental and its sub-harmonics.

4.4.4 \(f\)-Vector \(\to\) Just Intonation Ratios

Mapping M4: Simplex Counts to Harmony

The \(f\)-vector \((f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_d)\) encodes combinatorial information at each dimension. The ratios between consecutive entries determine just intonation intervals: \[ r_k = \frac{f_k}{f_{k-1}} \quad \text{for } k = 1, \ldots, d. \] These ratios tune the oscillator voices relative to the base frequency.

Example 4.5

The hollow tetrahedron (\(S^2\)) has \(\mathbf{f} = (4, 6, 4)\):

  • \(r_1 = 6/4 = 3/2\) — the perfect fifth.
  • \(r_2 = 4/6 = 2/3\) — the inverted fifth (a perfect fourth below).

A complex with \(\mathbf{f} = (5, 10, 10, 5)\) (the boundary of the 4-simplex) yields ratios \(2, 1, 1/2\): an octave, a unison, and a sub-octave.

Code Stitch: f-Vector to Ratios

/// Set the just-intonation ratios from the f-vector of the complex.
/// Mapping M4: f_vector -> harmonic ratios for oscillator tuning.
///
/// The ratio r_k = f_k / f_{k-1} becomes the frequency multiplier
/// for the k-th oscillator voice.
pub fn set_ratios_from_f_vector(&mut self, f_vector: &[usize]) {
    self.ratios.clear();
    self.ratios.push(1.0); // fundamental always at ratio 1
    for window in f_vector.windows(2) {
        if window[0] > 0 {
            // r_k = f_k / f_{k-1} (just intonation ratio)
            self.ratios.push(window[1] as f64 / window[0] as f64);
        }
    }

    // Retune oscillators according to computed ratios
    for (i, osc) in self.fundamentals.iter_mut().enumerate() {
        let ratio = self.ratios.get(i % self.ratios.len())
            .copied().unwrap_or(1.0);
        osc.set_frequency(self.base_freq * ratio);
    }
}

4.4.5 \(\chi \to\) Reverb Character (Gauss-Bonnet)

Theorem 4.6 (Gauss-Bonnet, discrete version)

For a closed orientable surface \(\Sigma\), the Gauss-Bonnet theorem states: \[ \int_\Sigma K \, dA = 2\pi \chi(\Sigma), \] where \(K\) is the Gaussian curvature. The Euler characteristic is a proxy for total curvature.

Mapping M5: Curvature to Reverb

The CurvatureReverb effect uses \(\chi\) to determine the reverb character:

  • \(\chi > 0\) (positive curvature, sphere-like): short RT60, bright damping. Geodesics on a sphere converge, so reflections focus quickly.
  • \(\chi = 0\) (flat, torus-like): medium RT60. Geodesics stay parallel.
  • \(\chi < 0\) (negative curvature, high-genus): long RT60, dark damping. Geodesics on a hyperbolic surface diverge, so reflections scatter widely.

Code Stitch: CurvatureReverb

/// Create a reverb parameterized by the Euler characteristic.
/// Implements Mapping M5 via the Gauss-Bonnet connection.
///
/// chi > 0 -> positive curvature -> short, bright reverb
/// chi = 0 -> flat -> medium reverb
/// chi < 0 -> negative curvature -> long, dark reverb
pub fn from_euler_characteristic(chi: isize, sample_rate: f64) -> Self {
    // Map curvature to RT60 (reverb time)
    let rt60 = match chi {
        c if c > 0 => 0.5 + 0.3 / c as f64,  // short
        0 => 1.5,                              // medium
        c => 1.5 + 0.5 * (-c as f64),          // long
    };

    // Damping coefficient: bright for spheres, dark for high genus
    let damping = match chi {
        c if c > 0 => 0.2,  // bright
        0 => 0.5,
        _ => 0.8,              // dark
    };

    // Comb filter delays: mutually prime for maximum density
    // (Schroeder reverb design — 8 parallel comb filters)
    let base_delay = (rt60 * sample_rate * 0.03) as usize;
    let comb_delays = [
        base_delay + 7, base_delay + 23, base_delay + 41,
        base_delay + 59, base_delay + 73, base_delay + 97,
        base_delay + 113, base_delay + 131,
    ];
    // ...
}

4.4.6 Persistence Pairs \(\to\) Delay Taps

Mapping M6: Persistence as Temporal Echo

Each persistence pair \((b, d, k)\) maps to a delay tap in the PersistenceDelay: \[ \text{delay\_time}_i = \frac{b_i}{\varepsilon_{\max}} \cdot T_{\max}, \quad \text{feedback}_i = \frac{d_i - b_i}{\varepsilon_{\max}}, \quad \text{level}_i = \frac{1}{1 + k_i}. \] Long-lived features (\(d - b\) large) produce more feedback (longer echoes). Higher-dimensional features (\(k\) large) are quieter.

Code Stitch: PersistenceDelay

/// Configure delay taps from persistence pairs.
/// Implements Mapping M6: (birth, death, dim) -> (delay, feedback, level).
pub fn set_from_persistence(
    &mut self,
    pairs: &[(f64, f64, usize)],  // (birth, death, dim)
    max_filtration: f64,
) {
    self.taps.clear();
    for &(birth, death, dim) in pairs {
        let persistence = if death.is_infinite() {
            max_filtration
        } else { death - birth };

        // birth -> delay time (proportional to filtration value)
        let delay_secs = (birth / max_filtration) * max_delay;
        let delay_samples = (delay_secs * sample_rate) as usize;

        // persistence -> feedback (long-lived features echo longer)
        let feedback = (persistence / max_filtration).clamp(0.0, 0.95);

        // dimension -> level (higher dim = quieter)
        let level = 1.0 / (1.0 + dim as f64);

        self.taps.push(DelayTap { delay_samples, feedback, level });
    }
}

4.4.7 Persistence \(\to\) Morse Score (MorseSynth)

Mapping M7: Filtration as Musical Score

The filtration parameter \(\varepsilon\) is mapped to time, and each birth/death event becomes a musical event: \[ t_{\text{event}} = \varepsilon \cdot s, \quad f_{\text{note}} = f_{\min} \cdot (f_{\max}/f_{\min})^{\varepsilon/\varepsilon_{\max}}, \] where \(s\) is a time-scale factor and the frequency mapping is logarithmic (perceptually uniform pitch spacing).

The waveform of each voice is determined by the homological dimension:

  • \(H_0\) births (component appears): sine wave — pure, simple, the genesis of a voice.
  • \(H_1\) births (loop forms): triangle wave — periodic but angular, reflecting the cycle.
  • \(H_2\) births (void closes): sawtooth wave — rich harmonics, the interior resonance.
  • Higher dimensions: square wave — maximum harmonic content.

4.5 The Karplus-Strong Algorithm

The SimplicialPluck instrument uses the Karplus-Strong algorithm, which is a discrete approximation to the 1D wave equation.

Definition 4.7 (Wave equation)

The 1D wave equation on an interval \([0, L]\) with fixed endpoints: \[ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad u(0, t) = u(L, t) = 0. \] The eigenmodes are \(u_n(x,t) = \sin(n\pi x / L) \cos(n\pi c t / L)\), with frequencies \(f_n = nc / (2L)\). This is the harmonic series.

Definition 4.8 (Karplus-Strong)

The Karplus-Strong algorithm approximates a plucked string using a delay line of length \(N = f_s / f_0\) samples (where \(f_0\) is the desired fundamental frequency) with a lowpass filter in the feedback loop: \[ y[n] = \frac{y[n - N] + y[n - N + 1]}{2} \cdot \alpha, \] where \(\alpha < 1\) is the damping coefficient. The delay line is initialized with the excitation signal (noise for a string pluck). The averaging operation acts as a first-order lowpass filter, which models the energy loss of higher harmonics—exactly what happens in a real vibrating string.

Our modification: instead of initializing the delay line with white noise, we use an excitation signal whose harmonic content is shaped by the \(f\)-vector: 

/// Excite the string with f-vector-weighted harmonics.
/// Instead of pure noise, the excitation is:
///   x[n] = 0.7 * Sum_k (f_k/total) * sin(2*pi*(k+1)*n/N) + 0.3 * noise[n]
///
/// This seeds the delay line with energy at f-vector-derived harmonics,
/// so the pluck sound reflects the combinatorial structure of K.
pub fn pluck(&mut self, f_vector: &[usize]) {
    self.active = true;
    let total: usize = f_vector.iter().sum::<usize>().max(1);

    for line in &mut self.delay_lines {
        for (i, sample) in line.iter_mut().enumerate() {
            let mut val = 0.0;
            for (k, &fk) in f_vector.iter().enumerate() {
                let weight = fk as f64 / total as f64;
                let harmonic = (k + 1) as f64;
                val += weight * (TAU * harmonic * i as f64
                    / line.len() as f64).sin();
            }
            // Add noise for naturalness
            let noise = /* LCG noise */;
            *sample = val * 0.7 + noise * 0.3;
        }
    }
}

4.6 Biquad Filters and Pole/Zero Topology

Definition 4.9 (Biquad transfer function)

A biquad filter has the transfer function: \[ H(z) = \frac{b_0 + b_1 z^{-1} + b_2 z^{-2}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + a_2 z^{-2}}, \] which has 2 zeros and 2 poles in the \(z\)-plane (\(\mathbb{C}\) or the Riemann sphere \(\hat{\mathbb{C}} \cong S^2\)). The filter is stable when all poles lie inside the unit disk \(|z| < 1\).

The topology of the pole/zero configuration determines the filter character. A pole near the unit circle at angle \(\theta\) creates a resonance at frequency \(f = \theta f_s / (2\pi)\). Moving the pole closer to the circle increases the resonance (sharper peak in the frequency response).

Our TopologicalFilter computes biquad coefficients from cutoff frequency and resonance (Q factor), which are in turn derived from topological parameters. The coefficient computation maps the parameter space \((\omega_c, Q) \in \mathbb{R}^+ \times \mathbb{R}^+\) to the coefficient space \((b_0, b_1, b_2, a_1, a_2) \in \mathbb{R}^5\)—a smooth map between manifolds.

4.7 Waveshaping as a Nonlinear Map

Definition 4.10 (Waveshaping)

A waveshaper is a memoryless nonlinear function \(g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) applied sample-by-sample: \(y[n] = g(x[n])\). If the input is a pure sine wave \(x[n] = A\sin(\omega n)\), the output can be expanded in a Chebyshev series: \[ g(A \sin\theta) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} c_k(A) \sin(k\theta), \] where the coefficients \(c_k(A)\) determine the harmonic content of the output. The waveshaping function determines which harmonics are generated.

The BettiDistortion uses a multi-stage wavefolding function: the input is passed through \(\beta_0\) stages of the sine wavefolder \(g(x) = \sin(\pi x / 2)\), which maps \(\mathbb{R}\) to \([-1, 1]\) with periodic wrapping. Additional asymmetric saturation (controlled by \(\beta_2\)) introduces even harmonics. 

/// Multi-stage wavefolding driven by Betti numbers.
/// beta_0 stages of sine wavefolding: g(x) = sin(pi*x/2).
/// Each fold adds harmonic complexity (new overtones).
pub fn process(&mut self, input: f64) -> f64 {
    let driven = (input + self.prev_sample * self.feedback) * self.drive;

    let mut x = driven;
    for _ in 0..self.fold_stages {
        // Sine wavefolder: R -> [-1, 1] with wrapping
        x = (x * FRAC_PI_2).sin();
    }

    // Asymmetric saturation from beta_2 (voids -> even harmonics)
    if self.asymmetry > 0.0 {
        x = if x >= 0.0 { x.tanh() }
            else { (x * (1.0 + self.asymmetry)).tanh()
                   / (1.0 + self.asymmetry) };
    }

    self.prev_sample = x;
    x
}

4.8 The Oscillator: Waveforms as Maps \(S^1 \to \mathbb{R}\)

A waveform is a periodic function of period \(T = 1/f\), equivalently a map from the circle \(S^1\) to the reals. Different waveforms correspond to different embeddings of the circle:

  • Sine: the projection of the standard embedding \(S^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^2\) onto the \(y\)-axis. Contains only the fundamental frequency. Topologically, the simplest periodic waveform.
  • Sawtooth: a map \(S^1 \to \mathbb{R}\) with a single discontinuity (jump). Contains all harmonics \(1/n\). The discontinuity makes it a degree-1 map between circles (wrapping once around the range).
  • Square: two discontinuities, only odd harmonics (\(1, 3, 5, \ldots\)). The \(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\) symmetry \(f(t + T/2) = -f(t)\) forces even harmonics to vanish—a spectral consequence of a topological symmetry.
  • Triangle: piecewise linear, \(1/n^2\) harmonic decay. Homeomorphic to the sine as a map \(S^1 \to \mathbb{R}\), but not smoothly so—the corners create higher harmonics.
/// PolyBLEP: polynomial band-limited step function for anti-aliasing.
/// At discontinuities (sawtooth jump, square transitions), the naive
/// waveform aliases. PolyBLEP subtracts a polynomial correction that
/// "smooths" the discontinuity while preserving the spectral content
/// below the Nyquist frequency.
fn poly_blep(&self, t: f64) -> f64 {
    let dt = self.phase_increment;
    if t < dt {
        let t = t / dt;
        2.0 * t - t * t - 1.0
    } else if t > 1.0 - dt {
        let t = (t - 1.0) / dt;
        t * t + 2.0 * t + 1.0
    } else { 0.0 }
}

4.10 The TopologySonifier: Putting It All Together

/// Create a sonifier from a simplicial complex.
/// This wires up all mappings M1-M7.
pub fn from_complex(
    complex: SimplicialComplex,
    base_freq: f64,
    sample_rate: f64,
) -> Self {
    // Compute invariants
    let betti = BettiNumbers::compute(&complex);      // Ch.2
    let chi = complex.euler_characteristic();         // Def 1.14
    let f_vec = complex.f_vector();                   // Def 1.9

    // Build instruments (Mappings M1-M4)
    let mut drone = BettiDrone::new(&betti, base_freq, sample_rate);
    drone.set_ratios_from_f_vector(&f_vec);            // M4

    let mut pluck = SimplicialPluck::new(base_freq, sample_rate);
    pluck.configure(base_freq, &f_vec, betti.b0(), betti.b1());

    let morse = MorseSynth::new(sample_rate);

    // Build effects (Mappings M5-M6)
    let delay = PersistenceDelay::new(sample_rate, 2.0);
    let reverb = CurvatureReverb::from_euler_characteristic(chi, sample_rate); // M5
    let distortion = BettiDistortion::from_betti(
        betti.b0(), betti.b1(), betti.b2()
    );

    Self { complex, betti, persistence: None,
           drone, pluck, morse, delay, reverb, distortion,
           drone_level: 0.5, pluck_level: 0.3, morse_level: 0.4,
           master_volume: 0.7, sample_rate }
}

Why Each Mapping Is Natural: Design Justifications

Design Justification: \(\beta_0 \to\) Oscillator Count

A connected component is, by definition, a maximal subspace within which any two points can be connected by a path. Two points in different components cannot interact. This is precisely the situation with independent oscillators: they produce independent signals that do not share harmonics or phase relationships.

The mapping is not merely analogical; it is categorically natural. The functor from simplicial complexes to their \(\pi_0\) (path components) is left adjoint to the discrete-space functor. Similarly, the “independent signal sources” functor from audio graphs preserves coproducts. The mapping \(\beta_0 \to\) voice count is the unique structure-preserving assignment that respects disjoint union: \(\beta_0(K_1 \sqcup K_2) = \beta_0(K_1) + \beta_0(K_2)\), and independent oscillators add linearly.

Design Justification: \(\beta_1 \to\) Modulation

A generator of \(H_1\) is a cycle — a closed path in the complex. Cycles are inherently periodic: traversing a loop returns you to the starting point. Low-frequency oscillators (LFOs) are the audio manifestation of periodicity below the audible range. The number of independent cycles (\(\beta_1\)) determines the number of independent modulation sources: each generator of \(H_1\) contributes its own periodic motion.

Physically: In vibrating membranes, the eigenmodes of the Laplacian on a surface with \(\beta_1 = g\) (genus \(g\)) include \(2g\) extra harmonic modes compared to the sphere. These extra modes correspond to vibrations “around the handles” and produce lower-frequency resonances — exactly the role our LFOs play.

Design Justification: \(\chi \to\) Reverb via Gauss-Bonnet

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem is one of the deepest results connecting geometry and topology: \[ \int_M K\, dA = 2\pi\chi(M). \] It says the total curvature of a closed surface is fixed by its topology. For sonification:

  • \(\chi > 0\) (sphere): positive curvature → geodesics converge → sound focuses → short, bright reverb (like a dome or parabolic reflector).
  • \(\chi = 0\) (torus): zero average curvature → geodesics stay parallel → neutral reverb (like a flat room).
  • \(\chi < 0\) (high-genus surface): negative curvature → geodesics diverge exponentially → sound scatters in many directions → long, dark, diffuse reverb (like a cave system).

This is not a metaphor. The wave equation on a Riemannian manifold \(M\) has Green's function \(G(x, y, t)\) whose decay rate is governed by the curvature of \(M\). Negative curvature produces slower decay (longer reverb tails). Our CurvatureReverb implements this principle via the discrete proxy of the Euler characteristic.

Design Justification: Persistence \(\to\) Delay Taps

A persistence pair \((b, d)\) records a topological feature that was born at scale \(b\) and died at scale \(d\). The duration \(d - b\) measures how “significant” the feature is — how wide a range of scales it survives.

In a delay effect, each tap echoes the input signal after some time. Our mapping: \(\mathrm{birth} \to \mathrm{delay\_time}\), \(\mathrm{persistence} \to \mathrm{feedback}\), \(\mathrm{dimension} \to \mathrm{level}\). Significant features echo longer (more feedback); short-lived features fade quickly. This is a temporal sonification of the persistence diagram: the delay effect literally “plays back” the topological history of the filtration.

Design Justification: Karplus-Strong as Discrete Wave Equation

The Karplus-Strong algorithm (1983) initializes a delay line with noise and averages adjacent samples in a feedback loop. This is a finite-difference discretization of the 1D wave equation \(\partial_{tt} u = c^2 \partial_{xx} u\) on an interval with periodic boundary conditions. The delay line length \(L = f_s / f_0\) sets the fundamental frequency.

On a simplicial complex, we replace the 1D Laplacian \(\partial_{xx}\) with the combinatorial Laplacian \(L_k = \partial_{k+1} \partial_{k+1}^T + \partial_k^T \partial_k\). The eigenvalues of \(L_0\) (the graph Laplacian) determine the natural frequencies of vibration on the complex. Our SimplicialPluck approximates this: the f-vector shapes the excitation spectrum, and the Betti numbers control the feedback filter, effectively tilting the spectral envelope toward the eigenfrequencies dictated by the topology.

References

Sonification and Auditory Display

  1. Hermann, T., Hunt, A., and Neuhoff, J.G. (eds.) The Sonification Handbook. Logos Verlag, 2011. ISBN 978-3-8325-2819-5.
    The standard reference for sonification design. Chapter 2 (taxonomy of sonification techniques), Chapter 5 (parameter mapping), and Chapter 15 (psychoacoustic considerations) are directly relevant to our design choices.
  2. Grond, F. and Berger, J. “Parameter mapping sonification.” In The Sonification Handbook, Chapter 15, pp. 363–397.
    Systematic treatment of mapping data dimensions to audio parameters. Our topology-to-audio mapping is an instance of their “many-to-many” mapping paradigm.

Spectral Graph Theory and the Laplacian

  1. Chung, F.R.K. Spectral Graph Theory. AMS CBMS 92, 1997.
    The graph Laplacian, its eigenvalues, Cheeger inequality, and connections to random walks. Chapter 1 defines the normalized Laplacian whose spectrum our filters approximate.
  2. Lim, L.-H. “Hodge Laplacians on graphs.” SIAM Review 62(3) (2020), 685–715.
    Extends the graph Laplacian to higher-dimensional simplicial complexes: the k-th Hodge Laplacian \(L_k = \partial_{k+1}\partial_{k+1}^T + \partial_k^T\partial_k\). Its kernel is isomorphic to \(H_k\) (discrete Hodge theorem). The eigenvalues determine the natural frequencies of k-dimensional vibration.
  3. Barbarossa, S. and Sardellitti, S. “Topological signal processing over simplicial complexes.” IEEE Trans. Signal Process. 68 (2020), 2992–3007.
    Signal processing on simplicial complexes using the Hodge decomposition. Signals decompose into gradient + curl + harmonic components; the harmonic component lives in the homology. Directly connects our topological invariants to signal processing.

Sound Synthesis References

  1. Karplus, K. and Strong, A. “Digital synthesis of plucked-string and drum timbres.” Computer Music Journal 7(2) (1983), 43–55.
    The original Karplus-Strong algorithm. Our SimplicialPluck extends this with topology-driven excitation signals and feedback filter parameters.
  2. Smith, J.O. Physical Audio Signal Processing. Online book, 2010. ccrma.stanford.edu/~jos/pasp/
    Comprehensive treatment of digital waveguides, biquad filters, and physical modeling synthesis. Our biquad filter implementation follows the Audio EQ Cookbook conventions referenced here.
  3. Välimäki, V., Pakarinen, J., Erkut, C., and Karjalainen, M. “Discrete-time modelling of musical instruments.” Rep. Prog. Phys. 69(1) (2006), 1–78.
    Survey of physical modeling synthesis techniques. Section 3 (string models) and Section 5 (resonator models) are relevant to our instrument design.

Morse Theory

  1. Milnor, J. Morse Theory. Annals of Mathematics Studies 51. Princeton, 1963.
    The classic. Chapters 1–3 develop the relationship between critical points and topology changes. Our MorseSynth implements the discrete analogue: as the filtration parameter sweeps, births and deaths of homological features correspond to critical points of the filtration function.
  2. Forman, R. “Morse theory for cell complexes.” Adv. Math. 134(1) (1998), 90–145.
    Discrete Morse theory on CW-complexes. Defines discrete Morse functions, gradient vector fields, and critical simplices. A future direction for enriching MorseSynth with true discrete Morse functions rather than filtration-derived approximations.
  3. Matsumoto, Y. An Introduction to Morse Theory. AMS Translations of Mathematical Monographs 208, 2002.
    Accessible introduction including the Morse inequalities \(c_k \geq \beta_k\) and the Morse-Smale complex. Well-suited for readers coming from the applied side.

Curvature and the Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

  1. do Carmo, M.P. Riemannian Geometry. Birkhäuser, 1992.
    Chapter 6 proves the Gauss-Bonnet theorem for surfaces. The connection between curvature and topology is the mathematical foundation of our CurvatureReverb.
  2. Forman, R. “Bochner's method for cell complexes and combinatorial Ricci curvature.” Discrete Comput. Geom. 29(3) (2003), 323–374.
    Defines Ricci curvature for cell complexes. A future refinement: instead of using the global Euler characteristic, use per-vertex combinatorial curvature for spatially varying reverb.
